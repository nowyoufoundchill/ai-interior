# Phase 2 Plan

## Title
Design Brain + Owner Flow Rebuild

## Date
2026-07-08

## Why this phase exists
The Release Gate passed, but the real owner test exposed the actual product gap:

- the app feels mechanically complete but emotionally and logically uneven
- the room-edit preservation is unusually strong
- the design taste, spatial judgment, and overall flow still feel off
- the current room journey over-emphasizes diagnosis and products before the user has approved a design direction

This plan takes the real test session seriously as the source of truth. It also incorporates the attached Claude review, especially its core finding: the pipeline's most owner-visible artifact, the render, is not yet backed by the same depth of design intelligence as diagnosis and concepts.

## What the real test showed

### What worked
- Vercel env configuration was correct; live provider calls succeeded.
- Diagnosis, concept generation, concept locking, and render execution all completed successfully in production.
- The render edit preserved architecture, lens perspective, wall geometry, windows, floor plane, and lighting with unusually high fidelity.
- The app proved that photo-preserving edits of real rooms are a genuine strength, not a mock-only illusion.

### What felt wrong
- The room workflow feels out of order for a homeowner.
- Diagnosis is too front-and-center in the user journey.
- Product sourcing was positioned too early relative to visual proof.
- Long-running actions looked stuck even when they succeeded.
- Spatial judgment is not reliable enough yet: one render placed a credenza in front of a door.
- Design taste is inconsistent: technically impressive edits can still feel generic or off.
- Product sourcing architecture drifted from the intended provider split and had to be corrected during the session.

### Product truth after the test
The app's differentiator is not "AI diagnosis." It is:

1. understanding a real room photo without destroying it
2. preserving spatial reality while editing it
3. turning an approved direction into believable before/after imagery

That means the user flow, design-brain architecture, and evaluation loop should all optimize for concept approval and photo transformation first.

### Visual benchmark truth
The owner has now provided a clearer bar for taste and presentation:

- the current app's layout and styling can feel like a high-school design presentation even when the image-edit technology is strong
- external examples show world-class render polish and stronger design maturity, even when they sometimes fail architectural preservation
- `IMG_1143.PNG` is the current directional benchmark for the room's intended taste level: calmer, more resolved, more editorial, and more like the work of an experienced Charleston-aware residential designer

The app therefore needs two upgrades at once:

1. preserve-real-room rendering discipline
2. a much more mature interface and design-language system around the outputs

## Phase goal
Make AI Interior Atelier feel like a real design intelligence system, not just a working AI pipeline, by:

- reshaping the room journey around concept approval and photo transformation
- upgrading the design brain from descriptive to constraint-enforcing
- improving taste and render direction quality
- reducing owner confusion during long-running steps
- moving product sourcing into a supporting role after visual direction is proven

## New product flow

### Current flow
Home -> Room -> Diagnosis -> Concepts -> Lock -> Products -> Renders -> Chat

### Target flow
Home -> Room -> Brief + Photos -> Concepts -> Approve Direction -> Apply to Photos -> Refine in Chat -> Products optional

### Product implications
- Diagnosis becomes a behind-the-scenes intelligence layer or a secondary tab, not the star of the experience.
- "Lock concept" becomes "Approve direction."
- Renders move ahead of Products in both logic and emphasis.
- Products become optional execution support after the owner likes the visual direction.
- Chat becomes a refinement surface tied to the approved direction and the latest photo edit.

## Phase streams

## Stream A: Room Flow + UX Reorder

### Objective
Make the room experience match the homeowner's natural mental model.

### Changes
- Reframe the primary room tabs and calls to action around:
  - Brief + Photos
  - Concepts
  - Apply to Photos
  - Chat
  - Products
- Demote Diagnosis from a required-feeling early stage to a supporting artifact.
- Replace "Lock concept" language with "Approve direction."
- Make the stage card and next-step guidance point to the render step immediately after concept approval.
- Reduce admin-panel feeling in the workspace by using more outcome language and less pipeline language.

### Acceptance criteria
- A first-time user can describe the room workflow as "pick a direction, see it on my room, then refine it."
- No stage card suggests Products before the owner has seen a render.
- The room page reads as a design experience, not a testing console.

## Stream A2: Interface Redesign System

### Objective
Rebuild the app's visual language so it feels like a senior interior design studio, not a student presentation board.

### Changes
- Redesign the room workspace, dashboard, concept cards, and render detail views around editorial hierarchy and restraint.
- Replace the current generic cream-and-card treatment with a more deliberate brand system:
  - luxury-neutral base tones
  - stronger contrast discipline
  - less default-box UI
  - more breathing room and better spatial rhythm
- Upgrade typography:
  - more intentional serif/sans pairing
  - better sizing hierarchy
  - more mature labels, captions, and artifact framing
- Rework concept presentation so it reads like a professional studio review:
  - concept name
  - short thesis
  - decisive palette/material story
  - fewer filler blocks
  - stronger visual hierarchy
- Redesign the render page so the image is the hero and the explanation supports it rather than competing with it.
- Create an "approved direction" visual state that feels premium and final.
- Reduce "pipeline UI" language and admin-table energy in status cards, empty states, and filters.
- Add a design-reference mode inspired by the stronger external examples:
  - optional annotated presentation boards
  - palette strips
  - layout notes
  - room rationale overlays
  - but never as the default replacement for the clean hero render

### Benchmark direction
- Use `IMG_1143.PNG` as the closest taste benchmark for this office.
- Borrow from the strongest external examples:
  - stronger composition
  - calmer palette presentation
  - more mature typography
  - more resolved furniture/styling hierarchy
- Do not borrow their weaknesses:
  - no architectural drift
  - no relocated doors
  - no fake room geometry

### Acceptance criteria
- The room workspace feels like a premium design studio product, not a prototype dashboard.
- The render page visually centers the room image first and the metadata second.
- Typography, color, spacing, and card systems feel intentional and brandable.
- A user should describe the app as polished, calm, and expert-led before they even read the AI output.

## Stream A3: UI/UX Redesign Program

### Objective
Define and implement a full interaction and information-architecture redesign, not just a visual refresh.

### Scope
- Dashboard
- Home detail
- Room workspace
- Concepts review and approval
- Render review and revision flow
- Design chat flow
- Products as an optional post-approval execution layer
- mobile and desktop behavior across the room journey

### Changes
- Redesign the information architecture of the room workspace so the next best action is always obvious.
- Replace the current tab-first workflow with clearer step framing, stronger progression cues, and more legible stage transitions.
- Clarify what is primary versus secondary on each screen:
  - approved direction
  - latest render
  - requested revisions
  - supporting diagnosis and product details
- Rebuild empty states, success states, waiting states, and stale states so they explain what is happening in normal language.
- Redesign concept approval UX so choosing a direction feels decisive and high-confidence.
- Redesign the render revision flow so it feels like collaborating with a designer, not submitting a background job.
- Make chat context-aware in the interface:
  - what concept is active
  - what render is current
  - what the user last asked to change
- Simplify and reframe products so they feel like a downstream execution layer rather than a competing main tab.
- Improve mobile layouts so critical actions and artifacts remain readable without feeling like squeezed desktop cards.

### Deliverables
- Updated user journey map for the full room flow
- Revised information architecture for the room workspace
- Wireframes for:
  - Dashboard
  - Room overview
  - Concepts approval
  - Render review
  - Chat revision flow
  - Optional products flow
- Component-level redesign rules for:
  - headers
  - stage cards
  - artifact cards
  - action bars
  - progress states
  - empty states
  - approval states
- Copy rewrite guidelines for owner-facing labels, instructions, and state messaging

### Acceptance criteria
- A first-time user can move through the room flow without needing the pipeline explained to them.
- Each room screen has a clear primary action and a clear secondary action.
- Waiting, success, stale, and revision states all feel intentional rather than incidental.
- Mobile and desktop layouts both preserve hierarchy and calmness.
- The app feels like a guided design service, not a stack of tool tabs.

## Stream B: Design Brain Constraint Engine

### Objective
Turn room understanding into hard design rules, not just descriptive output.

### Changes
- Expand room-intelligence output into explicit non-negotiable constraints:
  - doors and door swings
  - circulation paths
  - desk and chair clearances
  - window operation zones
  - camera/backdrop logic for video-call rooms
  - furniture placement no-go zones
- Pass those constraints into concept generation, render planning, and critique as hard blockers.
- Add a formal "layout violation" layer to critic output.
- Treat "do not block doors/windows/walkways" as a release-blocking design rule, not stylistic guidance.

### Acceptance criteria
- Render planning explicitly names clear zones and forbidden zones.
- Critics can flag circulation and blockage failures directly.
- The system should not approve a render instruction set that places casework or seating in front of a door or active circulation path.

## Stream C: Render Director Rebuild

### Objective
Upgrade the flagship artifact, the room render, to use the full design brain.

### Changes
- Implement the Claude review's central recommendation:
  - build and pass a full `context_brain` into `renderPromptDirector()`
  - include property dossier
  - include room intelligence
  - include design portfolio
  - include style library lighting layers and luxury mechanics
- Replace the thin render prompt with a v2 prompt written from a photographer/stylist point of view.
- Add a real Render Critic pass before or alongside image-edit execution.
- Add explicit preservation and constraint language for:
  - door clearance
  - path clearance
  - architectural fidelity
  - visible realism
  - no warped or duplicated objects

### Acceptance criteria
- Render prompts are visibly more specific, room-aware, and camera-aware.
- The render system explains what light is doing to materials, not just what objects to add.
- Render instructions must mention the exact architectural surfaces they are modifying.

## Stream D: Taste and Direction Quality

### Objective
Raise design taste so the app's styling quality catches up with its spatial edit quality.

### Changes
- Tune concept prompts and style-library usage to produce stronger points of view, not three mild variants of the same idea.
- Add voice and design-governance rules:
  - no generic luxury adjectives without mechanism
  - no decorative filler rationales
  - rationale must defend palette, layout, materials, and restraint choices
- Improve style-anchor selection so repeated runs do not collapse into the same weathered-oak coastal family unless the brief truly demands it.
- Add explicit rejection logic for regionally wrong or tacky moves, especially in coastal contexts.

### Acceptance criteria
- Concept directions read like defensible design positions, not re-skinned summaries.
- Owner feedback on concepts should focus on taste preference, not genericness.
- Distinct concepts should differ in spatial attitude, formality, contrast, and material logic, not just palette.
- Final approved directions should feel closer to `IMG_1143.PNG` than to generic "coastal office" Pinterest composites.

## Stream E: Long-Running Action UX

### Objective
Remove the feeling that the app is frozen or broken during live model calls.

### Changes
- Improve post-submit states for diagnosis, concepts, products, and renders.
- Show clear progress messaging for long-running live steps.
- Force a reliable UI refresh after successful long-running actions.
- Add timeout-aware messaging when the backend succeeds but the UI is still waiting.
- Normalize stage transitions so the owner sees what just completed and what is next.

### Acceptance criteria
- No successful live action should feel like it silently hung.
- The owner should always know whether the app is still working, succeeded, or needs retry.

## Stream F: Product Sourcing Role Reset

### Objective
Make products support the approved design rather than compete with the core experience.

### Changes
- Keep Tavily as the sourcing engine for product research in the live path.
- Position products after render approval in the user journey.
- Rework product copy so it reads like execution support for the approved direction.
- Validate whether the sourced products visually and practically match the approved room edit, not just the concept JSON.

### Acceptance criteria
- Products feel like "how to buy this room," not "another AI stage."
- Product sourcing no longer distracts from the concept-to-render loop.

## Stream G: Real Eval Harness

### Objective
Judge progress the way the owner actually experiences the app.

### Changes
- Define a recurring real-room evaluation loop:
  - create room
  - enter brief and dimensions
  - generate concepts
  - approve direction
  - render on real photos
  - request revisions in chat
- Capture owner feedback after each artifact:
  - concept quality
  - render preservation
  - render taste
  - design logic
  - workflow clarity
- Treat owner reaction as the gate for this phase, not automated suites alone.

### Acceptance criteria
- We can run one end-to-end live cycle and explain exactly what improved and what still failed.
- Success is measured by owner confidence and quality perception, not just route correctness.

- Move Renders ahead of Products in guidance and emphasis
- Demote Diagnosis in the visible journey

### Phase 2B

- Redesign typography, color system, layout rhythm, and card hierarchy
- Make the render page the visual hero
- Rework concept and approval presentation to feel premium

### Phase 2C
UI/UX redesign program
- Redesign information architecture and room journey interactions
- Produce wireframes and component behavior rules

### Phase 2D
Constraint engine
- Expand room-intelligence outputs
- Add explicit no-go zones and circulation rules
- Feed them into render and concept generation

### Phase 2E
Render Director rebuild
- Wire full context brain into render planning
- Land new render prompt
- Add Render Critic

### Phase 2F
Taste upgrade
- Tune concept prompt and style-anchor selection

### Phase 2G

- Keep Tavily live

Owner-judged live cycle
- Run the full loop on the office again
- Record owner reaction as the real gate

## Out of scope for this phase
- Multi-user auth
- public launch concerns
- destructive schema cleanup
- rebuilding the entire storage model
- adding more AI providers

## Risks
- Better wording alone will not fix spatial mistakes if constraints remain soft.
- Render quality may stay visually strong while still making logically wrong placement decisions unless critic enforcement is real.
- Moving products later in the flow will require UI and language edits across multiple surfaces.
- Owner taste quality is partly a design-library and prompt-governance problem, not a single-model problem.

## Recommended first build slice
The highest-leverage first slice is:

1. Reorder the visible room flow around Concepts -> Apply to Photos -> Chat -> Products
2. Redesign the room workspace and render presentation to feel premium and editorial
3. Add a real UI/UX redesign pass for IA, state design, and interaction clarity
4. Rebuild `renderPromptDirector()` on the full context brain
5. Add hard circulation and door-clearance rules to render planning

That is the smallest meaningful package most likely to change the owner's judgment of the app.

## Gate for completion
Phase 2 is complete when a real owner live cycle produces all of the following:

- the room flow feels intuitive
- the concept step feels like the main design decision
- the render preserves the room and respects circulation rules
- the design direction feels more intentional and less generic
- products feel optional and supportive rather than prematurely central
- the owner describes the app as feeling more like a design intelligence system than an AI pipeline
